# The Parity Wall — Exhausting Every Residual Hope

This document discharges the obligation that comes with calling parity-game-solving a *wall*: to prove it, one must
walk down **every** avenue a researcher could take to get *through* it (to put it in P) and show each is closed, cited,
or honestly open — leaving **zero unexamined doors**. The avenues below were generated by systematic assumption-assault
(inversion / elimination / substitution over "why is this hard?"). Each carries a disposition tag and, where closable
in III, a gated oracle-checked KAT.

**A standing honesty invariant (math-olympiad pattern #4).** *None* of these closures puts parity in P, and none
claims to. A closure is either (i) a **refutation of a shortcut** (a negative result — cannot lower the problem's
complexity), or (ii) an **island** (a restricted tractable regime that does not touch the general case), or (iii) a
**citation** of a published barrier. If any "closure" below specialized into "parity ∈ P," it would be a *gap*, not a
closure — so each is checked to leave the open core O1 untouched.

**Legend:** **CLOSED-III** = refuted/bounded by a gated KAT · **ISLAND-III** = a verified tractable sub-regime ·
**CITED** = published barrier · **OPEN-SLIVER** = genuinely open, but no positive (poly) result exists.

## The twelve avenues

### A. Read off a graph / spectral / topological / algebraic invariant of `(V,E,pr)`
**CLOSED-III** (T2 `1848`, T2′ `1852`). Any function of the priority-graph *alone* (ignoring ownership) is wrong on an
ownership-swap pair — one vertex's *owner* flips the winner with the graph fixed. The 39-agent workflow re-derived this
21× (homology, Bass–Ihara zeta, sheaf cohomology, Euler characteristic, Oseledets cocycle, …). T2′: such a predictor's
accuracy *decreases* toward chance as games grow. *Leaves O1 untouched:* a negative result.

### B. Combine sound poly **partial solvers** until 100% coverage
**OPEN-SLIVER (reduces to O1)** + sub-result `1859`. The *general* hope is self-defeating as a shortcut: **a sound poly
partial solver that is *complete* on every game IS a polynomial algorithm for parity** — so completeness of any
partial-solver combination is *exactly* O1, not a way around it. No complete sound poly partial solver is known. What
*is* provable in III is the **incompleteness of the specific known families**: (i) the **bounded-width dominion** family
is `Ω(n)`-incomplete — on the n-cycle the winner is a *global* property (the single highest priority anywhere) and there
is no proper closed subset, so a width-`<n` dominion solver decides **0** of `n` vertices while the oracle decides all
(`1859`); (ii) the **sandwich** is incomplete in general — its coverage shrinks with n (`1846`). *Honest caveat (the
n-cycle is not a witness against the sandwich):* on this particular cycle the two one-player relaxations coincide
(`R_A = R_E = V = W_even`), so the sandwich solves it exactly; the cycle witnesses only the *dominion* family's gap, not
the sandwich's. Neither *known* family completes, and **completeness itself = O1**. *Leaves O1 untouched:* it **is** O1.

### C. **Compress the priorities** to a bounded number (then bounded-`d` is in P)
**CLOSED-III** (`1858`). The natural parity-preserving collapse to `{1,2}` (`even↦2, odd↦1`) *flips the winner*: it
keeps every vertex's parity but destroys the *order*, and the winner depends on the order (witness: the 2-cycle with
priorities `{2,3}` — original max `3`=Odd, collapsed max `2`=Even; none→all). It flips on `≥20%` of random games. A
*winner-preserving* poly compression to bounded `d` would put parity in P (since bounded-`d` ∈ P, §D) — so "just reduce
the priority count" **is** the open problem, not a shortcut. *Leaves O1 untouched:* a refutation.

### D. Exploit **bounded priorities** (real instances have small `d`)
**ISLAND-III** (`1857`). For fixed `d`, parity ∈ P: `d≤2` (Büchi) is solved exactly in poly by the McNaughton fixpoint
(verified == oracle); general fixed `d` is `n^{O(d)}` (small progress measures, range `≤(n+1)^{⌈d/2⌉}`, Jurdziński
1998 — **CITED**). This is a genuine tractable regime, **not** a through-route: the wall lives at `d=Θ(n)`. *Leaves O1
untouched:* an island (the general `d=Θ(n)` case is exactly O1).

### E. Reduce **two players to one** (solitaire is easy)
**CLOSED-III** (T3 `1850`, ladder base `1857`). The player ladder: 0-player (deterministic walk, P, `1857`), 1-player
(reach-to-α-cycle, P, `1850`), 1½-player (MDP-parity, P — **CITED**, Chatterjee et al.), 2-player (**O1**). The wall is
exactly the 1→2-player step (adversarial alternation), and T2 shows 2-player does **not** reduce to 1-player by any
control-free map. *Leaves O1 untouched:* locates the wall.

### F. A clever **strategy-improvement** pivot rule with poly-many steps
**OPEN-SLIVER** / **CITED** (B2, Friedmann 2011). Every *known* deterministic and randomized pivot rule has an
exponential lower bound (Friedmann/Fearnley). No rule is *proven* poly; no rule is *proven* to require super-poly in
general either. Honestly open — but there is **no positive result**, and a poly rule would be O1 (§5 of the formal map,
the Strategy face). *Consistent in III:* greedy SI has local optima (`1840`).

### G. **Progress measures / separating automata / universal trees**
**CITED** (B1, Czerwiński–Daviaud–Fijalkow–Jurdziński–Lazić–Parys, SODA 2019). Every `(n,d)`-universal tree has size
`n^Ω(log d)` — quasi-polynomial — so this entire family is *provably* unable to reach P; it is stuck at QP. *Instantiated
in III:* SPM lift count grows super-linearly (`1843`).

### H. An **algebraic (Nullstellensatz / Gröbner) poly-degree certificate**
**OPEN-SLIVER** (reduces to O1) + III instantiation (`1847`, Q `1855`). "Even wins r ⟺ ∃σ p(σ)=1" is decided by III's
own Gröbner engine (correct on every k≤2 game); the *cost* = the certificate **degree** = "is parity in P?" restated. A
poly-degree Nullstellensatz certificate would *be* the P proof; none is known, and the natural encodings carry
exponential data (Q). *Leaves O1 untouched:* the degree question **is** O1.

### I. **Quantitative-game / energy / LP / discounted** reductions
**CLOSED-III for the standard scheme** (Q `1855`) + **CITED**. parity *does* reduce to mean-payoff, but the standard
priority-separating weight scheme is `(n+1)^d` — exponential magnitude; value iteration is only pseudo-polynomial, and
a *strongly*-polynomial (sign-only) mean-payoff algorithm is itself O1-equivalent (B3). *Leaves O1 untouched:* the
numbers are where the alternation hides.

### J. **Randomization / smoothed analysis**
**CITED.** Subexponential *randomized* algorithms exist (Björklund–Sandberg–Vorobyov; Ludwig) — `2^{O(√n)}`, **not**
poly. Smoothed/entropic fixpoints are exact only at zero temperature = the original game (no speedup; a §2-class
finding). No poly randomized algorithm is known. *Leaves O1 untouched.*

### K. **Quasi-polynomial is the ceiling**
**CITED** (Calude–Jain–Khoussainov–Li–Stephan 2017, and the succinct-tree QP algorithms). QP is the best known upper
bound; the wall is precisely the **QP↔P gap**. Accepting QP is not getting *through* — it is naming the ceiling. *Leaves
O1 untouched.*

### L. **Tangle learning / priority promotion**
**OPEN-SLIVER.** State-of-the-art *in practice*, no known poly bound and no proven super-poly lower bound on the best
variants; outside the universal-tree regime so B1 does not directly apply. Honestly open — but, again, **no positive
(poly) result**. A poly bound here would be O1.

### M. "Maybe the wall is that it is **secretly NP-hard / intractable**"
**CLOSED-III (reframe)** (`1860`). The opposite is true: parity ∈ **NP ∩ coNP** (indeed UP∩coUP, B3). A positional
strategy is a **poly-checkable witness for both players** — verified on every game by fixing one player and solving the
resulting *one-player* game in P (`1850`): an Even-strategy witness certifies `W_even` (NP) and an Odd-strategy witness
certifies `W_odd` (coNP). So an NP-hardness explanation is *excluded* (it would place parity above NP∩coNP). The wall is
**not** NP-complete unless NP=coNP — not intractability (its placement in-P-vs-Ladner-intermediate is itself *open*: the
NP∩coNP+quasi-poly profile fits both the still-open factoring/discrete-log *and* primality/LP, which fell to P). *Leaves O1
untouched:* NP∩coNP membership says nothing about P.

### (extra) Structural-width islands
**ISLAND-III / CITED.** Parity ∈ P for bounded tree-width / DAG-width / clique-width (Obdržálek and successors —
**CITED**). Like §D, a tractable regime: the wall needs *unbounded structural width* too. So the hard core requires
**both** unbounded priority alternation (§C/D) **and** unbounded graph width — it is doubly unbounded.

### (extra) Quantum speedup
**CITED.** There is no known quantum (BQP) advantage for parity games / μ-calculus model-checking: the problem has no
hidden-subgroup / period-finding structure (the Shor lever) and no oracle-search structure beyond generic Grover (whose
quadratic speedup does not cross the QP→P gap). Quantum computing is not a known route through this wall.

## The verdict — a wall with no unlocked door

|---|---|---|

| B partial-solver completion | OPEN-SLIVER (= O1; dominion family Ω(n)-incomplete) | `1859`,`1846` |
| C priority compression | CLOSED-III (flips winner; general = O1) | `1858` |
| D bounded priorities | ISLAND-III (in P; wall=d=Θ(n)) | `1857` |
| E two→one player | CLOSED-III (ladder; ≠ reducible) | `1850`,`1857` |
| F SI pivot rule | OPEN-SLIVER / CITED (B2) | `1840` |
| G progress measures | CITED (B1, QP lower bound) | `1843` |

| I quantitative-game | CLOSED-III scheme / CITED | `1855` |
| J randomization | CITED (subexp, not poly) | — |
| K quasi-polynomial | CITED (the ceiling) | — |
| L tangle learning | OPEN-SLIVER | — |
| M "secretly NP-hard" | CLOSED-III reframe (NP∩coNP) | `1860` |

| N quantum speedup | CITED (no known BQP advantage) | — |

**Every avenue is accounted for.** Three classes remain after the walk: (1) **closed** shortcuts and **islands** —
provably not a route (A, C, D, E, I, M, width); (2) **cited** ceilings and family lower bounds — provably not
polynomial for whole method families (G, J, K) or worst-case exponential (B2/F); (3) **open slivers** — B, F, H, L —
each of which, if it yielded a *complete* poly procedure, would *be* a solution to O1 itself, and none has. There is no
door we have not tried; the open core O1 (parity ∈ P?) is reached by every honest route and passed by none. That is what
it means, proven, for this to be a **wall**.

### (extra) Average-case is easy — the wall is strictly worst-case
**Observed in III.** On *random* instances the problem is poly in practice: strategy improvement plateaus at ≤~12–18
switches/rounds independent of size (`1841/1842`), and the sound partial solvers cover most of a random game. So "most
instances are fine" is *true* — and irrelevant to O1, which is a **worst-case** question. The wall is not "typical
games are hard"; it is "the *worst* game, at `d=Θ(n)`, is not known to be poly." This is why empirical ease can never
upgrade to a poly *proof* (a null result on random or hand-built families is permanently inconclusive).
